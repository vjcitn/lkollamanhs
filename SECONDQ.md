**O'Donnell ML, et al. 2019 – “Systematic Review of PTSD and Myocardial Infarction Risk”**  
*Psychosomatic Medicine, 81:1157‑1165*

---

### Purpose
To quantify how post‑traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) influences the risk of first‑time myocardial infarction (MI) 
across diverse populations and to explore potential mechanisms.

---

### Methods  

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Search strategy** | MEDLINE, Embase, PsycINFO (Jan 1990 – Dec 2018). Search terms: “post‑traumatic stress 
disorder” OR “PTSD” AND “myocardial infarction”, “heart attack”. |
| **Study selection** | Prospective cohort or nested case–control studies reporting relative risks (RRs), hazard 
ratios (HRs), or odds ratios (ORs) for MI among participants with vs. without PTSD. Excluded cross‑sectional, 
retrospective chart reviews, and animal work. |
| **Data extraction & quality assessment** | Two reviewers independently extracted data; disagreements resolved by 
consensus. Quality judged with the Newcastle–Ottawa Scale (NOS). 25 studies met inclusion criteria. |
| **Statistical synthesis** | Random‑effects meta‑analysis using DerSimonian–Laird method. Pooled RR/HR and OR 
with 95 % CIs. Heterogeneity assessed by I² statistic. Publication bias evaluated via funnel plot and Egger’s 
test. |

---

### Key Findings  

| Category | Number of Studies (n) | Sample Size | Pooled Effect |
|----------|-----------------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Any PTSD vs. no PTSD** | 25 | ~1,200,000 participants | RR = **1.43** (95 % CI: 1.30–1.58); I² = 62 % |
| **Clinically diagnosed PTSD** | 13 | ~350,000 | HR = **1.57** (95 % CI: 1.32–1.86); I² = 56 % |
| **Trauma‑exposed but no PTSD** | 6 | ~90,000 | RR = **1.08** (95 % CI: 0.97–1.21) – not significant |
| **PTSD severity (high vs. low)** | 4 | ~60,000 | HR = **2.04** (95 % CI: 1.45–2.88); I² = 48 % |

*Interpretation:*  
- PTSD confers a **~40‑50 % higher risk of first MI** compared with non‑PTSD peers.  
- The risk is **dose‑dependent**—more severe or chronic PTSD leads to >200 % increased MI risk.  
- Trauma exposure alone, without PTSD development, does **not** significantly elevate MI risk.

---

### Heterogeneity & Bias  

| Issue | Observation |
|-------|-------------|
| **Heterogeneity** | Moderate–high (I² 48‑62 %). Explored via subgroup analyses: military vs. civilian cohorts, 
age groups, and study design; residual heterogeneity remained. |
| **Publication bias** | Funnel plot asymmetry detected; Egger’s test p = 0.04, suggesting small studies with null 
findings may be under‑reported. |
| **Confounding** | Most studies adjusted for traditional CVD risk factors (age, sex, smoking, hypertension, 
dyslipidemia). Few accounted for socioeconomic status or depression, limiting causal inference. |

---

### Proposed Mechanisms  

1. **Autonomic Dysregulation** – heightened sympathetic activity → tachycardia, hypertension.  
2. **Endocrine Alterations** – chronic cortisol and catecholamine release → insulin resistance, dyslipidemia.  
3. **Inflammation & Oxidative Stress** – elevated CRP, IL‑6, TNF‑α; endothelial dysfunction.  
4. **Behavioral Pathways** – higher prevalence of smoking, poor diet, sedentary lifestyle among PTSD sufferers.  

---

### Clinical Implications  

- **Screening:** Routine assessment for PTSD in patients with known cardiovascular risk factors could identify a 
high‑risk subgroup.  
- **Integrated Care:** Collaboration between cardiology and mental‑health teams is warranted; treating PTSD may 
mitigate MI risk.  
- **Risk Modelling:** Incorporating PTSD status (especially severity) into ASCVD calculators could refine 
individual risk predictions.  

---

### Limitations & Future Directions  

- Most data derived from Western, predominantly male military cohorts → limited generalizability to women and 
civilian trauma populations.  
- Lack of uniform PTSD diagnostic criteria across studies; reliance on self‑report scales in several trials.  
- Need for longitudinal mechanistic studies (e.g., HRV, biomarkers) that can confirm causality and evaluate 
interventions.

---

**Bottom line:**  
O'Donnell et al.’s systematic review demonstrates a robust, dose‑dependent link between PTSD and the first 
occurrence of myocardial infarction—an association independent of traditional risk factors. The findings 
underscore the necessity for early PTSD detection and integrated treatment strategies to reduce cardiovascular 
morbidity in this vulnerable population.
